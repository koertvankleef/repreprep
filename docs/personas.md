# Research-Informed Proto-Personas for a Routine-Based Strength App

These are proto-personas, not validated personas. They are meant to guide early product decisions for an English-first, US-leaning workout app. They should be refined later through user interviews, usability testing, onboarding analytics, and retention data.

The product direction suggested by these personas is not “bodybuilding app” and not “generic wellness tracker”. A working hypothesis is:

**A calm, routine-first strength training app that helps people know what to do, execute it with minimal friction, log it quickly, and return consistently.**

The current colour palette appears compatible with this direction: the indigo action scale may provide enough technical/active identity, while the mint accent scale may keep it calm, inclusive, and less gender-coded.

# Persona 1 — Maya, the Habit Builder

## Snapshot

**Name:** Maya Carter
**Age:** 31
**Location:** Chicago, Illinois
**Gender:** Woman
**Occupation:** Product marketer at a mid-sized tech company
**Life context:** Busy, urban, hybrid work, socially active, limited spare time
**Fitness level:** Beginner-to-intermediate
**Training context:** Apartment gym, occasional home workouts, sometimes a basic commercial gym
**Primary goal:** Build and maintain a repeatable strength routine without overthinking it
**Persona type:** Primary target user
**Behavioural archetype:** Habit Builder

## Defining quote

“I don’t need a motivational speech. I need to open the app, start the workout, see what I did last time, and log today’s sets before I lose focus.”

## Why this persona matters

Maya represents the highest-likelihood early user for an English-first mobile workout app: smartphone-native, familiar with apps, open to fitness tracking, but not necessarily loyal to one system yet.

She is not a bodybuilder. She is not a complete beginner either. She knows strength training is useful, and she has enough experience to understand exercises like squats, rows, dumbbell presses, and planks. But her consistency depends heavily on friction.

For Maya, the app does not win by having the biggest exercise database. It wins by making the next workout feel obvious.

## Background and situation

Maya has tried several approaches: YouTube workouts, saved Instagram routines, gym machines, dumbbell workouts, maybe a popular fitness app or two. She starts strong, but routines often degrade because the system becomes too much work to maintain.

She may work out three or four times in a good week, then miss several sessions during a busy period. She dislikes the feeling that every return is a restart.

She is comfortable with apps and subscriptions, but skeptical of apps that overpromise transformation. She wants something competent, polished, and respectful of her time.

## Goals

Her practical goals are to:

* Start workouts quickly.
* Know which exercise comes next.
* Log sets, reps, and weights with very little effort.
* See what she did last time.
* Build consistency over weeks.
* Feel stronger and more toned.
* Avoid routines that require too much planning.

Her emotional goals are to:

* Feel capable.
* Feel organised.
* Feel like she is becoming “someone who trains”.
* Avoid shame when she misses a workout.
* Avoid being spoken to like either a gym bro or a weight-loss project.

## Motivations

Maya is motivated by visible and felt progress. She likes noticing that a dumbbell feels lighter, that she can do more reps, that her posture feels better, or that she did not skip the gym after work.

Her motivation is practical, not ideological. She does not want fitness to become her identity. She wants it to fit into her life.

## Key behaviours

Maya usually opens the app right before training. She does not want to configure anything at that moment.

During the workout she may be sweaty, distracted, low on patience, holding equipment, or moving between machines. She wants large controls, obvious next actions, and minimal navigation.

After the workout, she may glance at a summary if it feels rewarding, but she probably will not spend ten minutes analysing charts.

## Key tasks

1. Start today’s workout fast.
2. Log sets and reps with minimal taps.
3. Adjust weight or reps when needed.
4. See last performance for the same exercise.
5. Skip or substitute an exercise without breaking the workout.
6. Finish the workout and feel a clean sense of completion.
7. Return after missed days without feeling punished.

## Pain points

Maya’s biggest pain points are:

* Too many taps during active logging.
* Exercise apps that become admin systems.
* Not remembering what weight she used last time.
* Having to rebuild routines after small changes.
* Rigid plans that make missed workouts feel like failure.
* Loud, aggressive, or gender-coded fitness language.
* Apps that overemphasise calories, weight loss, streaks, or guilt.

## Needs from the app

Maya needs the app to behave like a calm workout assistant.

It should know the routine, show the next step, remember previous performance, and make logging almost effortless. It should keep the active workout screen free from navigation clutter. It should let her recover from interruptions gracefully.

The design principle for Maya is:

**During a workout, every extra tap is a tax on consistency.**

## Feature priorities

High priority:

* “Start workout” as a very prominent action.
* Workout mode with minimal chrome.
* Large rep/set controls.
* One-tap or low-tap access to previous performance.
* Clear current exercise, current set, and next set.
* Easy skip/substitute flow.
* Simple workout completion summary.
* Gentle return path after missed sessions.

Medium priority:

* Routine templates.
* Basic progression hints.
* Optional notes per exercise.
* Exercise instructions with concise form cues.
* Rest timer.

Low priority for initial version:

* Complex periodisation.
* Social sharing.
* Advanced analytics.
* Body measurements.
* Calorie tracking.

## Accessibility considerations

Maya may use the app in poor workout conditions: bright gym lighting, one-handed, in motion, tired, or while wearing earbuds. Tap targets must be large. Text hierarchy must be obvious. The app should not rely on colour alone to communicate completion, warning, or progress.

## Product risks

The product will fail Maya if it feels like a planning tool rather than a workout tool. She may appreciate structure, but she does not want to manage a system while training.

The product will also fail if it leans too hard into either “pink wellness” or “hardcore lifting”. Maya wants strength without identity theatre.

## Success metrics

Good signs:

* She starts a workout within seconds of opening the app.
* She logs most sets without editing text manually.
* She completes two or more workouts per week for several weeks.
* She returns after missed sessions.
* She checks previous performance during workouts.
* She keeps the default routine long enough to form a habit.

## Validation questions

* What makes her stop using current workout apps?
* How often does she need to change a routine?
* Does she prefer workout-first or routine-first landing?
* What information does she need during a set?
* What feels motivating versus judgmental?
* Does she want reminders, or do reminders create guilt?

## Confidence level

**High.** This is likely the strongest primary persona for the first version of the app.

# Persona 2 — Marcus, the Program Tweaker

## Snapshot

**Name:** Marcus Reed
**Age:** 38
**Location:** Austin, Texas
**Gender:** Man
**Occupation:** Software engineer / technical lead
**Life context:** Busy but structured; values systems and measurable progress
**Fitness level:** Intermediate
**Training context:** Commercial gym, home dumbbells as fallback
**Primary goal:** Maintain and refine strength routines over time
**Persona type:** Secondary power user
**Behavioural archetype:** Program Tweaker

## Defining quote

“I already know what I’m trying to train. I need the app to respect my structure and not fight me when I change the plan.”

## Why this persona matters

Marcus is not necessarily the largest audience, but he is important because he stress-tests the app’s information architecture. If routines, exercises, workout history, and progression are conceptually muddled, Marcus will feel it first.

He is also the persona most likely to expose whether the app has lasting value after the first few weeks.

## Background and situation

Marcus has trained on and off for years. He understands basic strength concepts: progressive overload, push/pull balance, leg work, rest, warm-up sets, and exercise variations.

He may not follow a formal bodybuilding programme, but he does think in blocks: current routine, next routine, deload week, substitution, weak point, variation, injury workaround.

He is not looking for the app to teach him fitness from scratch. He wants it to help him organise and execute what he already mostly understands.

## Goals

His practical goals are to:

* Create routines.
* Edit exercises and exercise order.
* Track weight, reps, sets, and progress.
* Compare intended routine structure with actual completed workouts.
* Rotate variations.
* Maintain a useful long-term training history.
* Make adjustments without corrupting past data.

His emotional goals are to:

* Feel in control.
* Trust that his data is coherent.
* Avoid fighting the app’s model.
* Feel that the app scales with him as he becomes more serious.

## Motivations

Marcus is motivated by progression and structure. He likes seeing that his dumbbell row improved, that his shoulder press is no longer stuck, or that he has trained consistently for a month.

He may enjoy charts more than Maya, but only if they are meaningful. He does not need vanity graphs. He wants useful feedback.

## Key behaviours

Marcus may open the app outside workouts to edit a routine, add an exercise, review history, or plan the next training block.

Unlike Maya, he does not only use the app during active exercise. He uses it in two distinct modes:

1. **Planning mode** — calm, reflective, more detail-tolerant.
2. **Workout mode** — focused, fast, minimal friction.

This distinction should strongly influence navigation and layout.

## Key tasks

1. Create a new routine.
2. Rename and reorder exercises.
3. Add exercise variations.
4. Edit sets, rep targets, and weights.
5. Start a planned workout.
6. Log actual performance.
7. Review previous performance by exercise.
8. Compare routine intent against what actually happened.
9. Adjust the routine for next time.

## Pain points

Marcus’s biggest pain points are:

* Apps that mix planning and active workout UX.
* Routine editing that feels cramped on mobile.
* Poor distinction between routine templates and completed workouts.
* Inability to preserve history when changing a routine.
* Exercise lists that are either too generic or too hard to customise.
* Navigation where settings, utilities, and core workout flows are mixed together.
* Data models that do not handle “same exercise, different routine” cleanly.

## Needs from the app

Marcus needs the app to have a strong conceptual model.

He should be able to understand the difference between:

* Routine.
* Workout session.
* Exercise.
* Exercise variation.
* Set target.
* Completed set.
* Historical performance.

The design principle for Marcus is:

**Planning can be rich; workout execution must stay lean.**

## Feature priorities

High priority:

* Clear routine/exercise hierarchy.
* Dedicated routine editing flow.
* Distinction between planned and completed work.
* Ability to reorder exercises.
* Ability to adjust targets.
* Exercise history.
* Previous performance per exercise.
* Preservation of historical logs after routine edits.

Medium priority:

* Exercise variations.
* Duplicate routine.
* Archive routine.
* Routine notes.
* Training block notes.
* Progress views by exercise and routine.
* Desktop/tablet-friendly planning layout.

Low priority for initial version:

* Advanced periodisation automation.
* AI-generated programmes.
* Public programme marketplace.
* Complex body metrics.
* Social feeds.

## Navigation implications

Marcus strongly supports the idea that the app needs a clear root/detail hierarchy.

On mobile, he needs obvious paths between:

* Routines.
* Exercises.
* Workout history.
* Active workout.
* Options/settings.

On wider layouts, he benefits from a sidebar or rail because planning involves moving between contexts. On mobile, that same complexity should not be present during active logging.

## Accessibility considerations

Marcus may not personally need larger text or accessibility settings, but his use case exposes layout density problems. Routine editing screens can easily become cramped. If the app handles Marcus’s information density while still respecting text scaling, the design will be much more robust.

## Product risks

The product will fail Marcus if it is too simplistic. “Just tap reps and move on” is enough for Maya but not for him.

It will also fail if it becomes too complex during workout mode. Marcus wants power, but not at the cost of execution speed.

## Success metrics

Good signs:

* He creates or edits more than one routine.
* He reviews exercise history.
* He uses previous performance during workouts.
* He adjusts routines without abandoning the app.
* He keeps using the app beyond the initial routine.
* He trusts the app’s handling of historical data.

## Validation questions

* How often does he edit routines?
* Does he think in “routine versions” or simply “current routine”?
* How much history does he expect to survive routine changes?
* Does he want exercise variations grouped together?
* What progress view is actually useful?
* Where does planning belong on mobile versus desktop?

## Confidence level

**Medium-high.** This persona is very useful for architecture and IA decisions, even if he may not be the first mainstream user.

# Persona 3 — Angela, the Long-Term Health Steward

## Snapshot

**Name:** Angela Brooks
**Age:** 57
**Location:** Raleigh, North Carolina
**Gender:** Woman
**Occupation:** School administrator
**Life context:** Full life, some caregiving responsibilities, wants to age well
**Fitness level:** Returning beginner
**Training context:** Home workouts, walking, occasional gym, possible physiotherapist advice
**Primary goal:** Build sustainable strength for health, mobility, and confidence
**Persona type:** Inclusion, retention, trust, and accessibility persona
**Behavioural archetype:** Long-Term Health Steward

## Defining quote

“I want to get stronger safely, and I want to know my progress won’t disappear when I change phones.”

## Why this persona matters

Angela prevents the product from becoming too young, too intense, too aesthetic, or too short-term.

She is not a “senior fitness” stereotype. She is still working, busy, capable, and digitally competent. But her needs are different from Maya’s and Marcus’s. She cares less about optimising a programme and more about safety, clarity, continuity, and confidence.

This persona also absorbs the genuinely useful part of Copilot’s “Data Guardian”: long-term trust, backup, readability, and recoverability.

## Background and situation

Angela knows strength training matters more now than it used to. She wants stronger legs, better posture, better balance, more energy, and fewer “I shouldn’t lift that” moments.

She may have had knee, shoulder, hip, or lower-back discomfort in the past. She is not necessarily injured, but she is careful. She wants guidance that respects caution without treating her as fragile.

She may use a smartwatch, health app, or step counter, but she does not necessarily trust every app with her data. She has lost data before when changing devices or abandoning apps. She wants to feel that what she logs is hers.

## Goals

Her practical goals are to:

* Start with safe, manageable routines.
* Understand what each exercise is for.
* Avoid injury.
* Track progress over months and years.
* Keep workouts simple enough to repeat.
* Use larger text comfortably.
* Back up or export her data.
* Adjust the app to her readability needs.

Her emotional goals are to:

* Feel safe.
* Feel respected.
* Feel independent.
* Feel that she is investing in her future self.
* Avoid feeling patronised.
* Avoid being pushed into unsafe intensity.

## Motivations

Angela is motivated by capability and independence. She wants to carry groceries, climb stairs, travel, garden, play with grandchildren or nieces/nephews, and move through daily life with confidence.

Aesthetics may still matter, but they are not the primary driver. Strength is framed as freedom.

## Key behaviours

Angela may train two or three shorter sessions per week. She may combine strength with walking, swimming, mobility, or stretching.

She is likely to read instructions more carefully than Maya. She may use exercise guidance before starting a movement. She may stop if something feels wrong and want to record that.

She may not explore every feature, but the features she does use must be clear and reliable.

## Key tasks

1. Start a beginner-safe routine.
2. Read or view form guidance.
3. Log sets with simple controls.
4. Mark an exercise as uncomfortable or painful.
5. Choose an easier alternative.
6. Increase gradually over time.
7. Review long-term consistency.
8. Adjust text size, contrast, or theme.
9. Export or back up data.
10. Restore data on a new device.

## Pain points

Angela’s biggest pain points are:

* Apps that assume high intensity.
* Tiny controls and dense screens.
* Exercise explanations that are too vague.
* Fitness language that feels shaming or age-coded.
* Not knowing the difference between muscle effort and joint pain.
* Losing data when changing devices.
* Backup/export hidden under obscure settings.
* Accessibility settings scattered across the app.
* Layouts that break when text size is increased.

## Needs from the app

Angela needs the app to feel safe, legible, and trustworthy.

It should provide beginner-safe defaults, clear form cues, alternative exercises, and a way to record discomfort. It should honour device text scaling. It should have an obvious Data & Backup section. It should make long-term history feel durable.

The design principle for Angela is:

**Trust is a feature, not polish.**

## Feature priorities

High priority:

* Beginner-safe routines.
* Clear exercise guidance.
* Easier alternatives.
* Pain/discomfort notes.
* Large readable workout screens.
* Text-scale resilient layouts.
* Data export.
* Backup/restore.
* Clear settings/options panel.
* Calm, non-shaming progress views.

Medium priority:

* Recovery prompts.
* Gentle reminders.
* Long-term history.
* Functional progress framing.
* Optional wearable import.
* Accessibility preview/testing.

Low priority for initial version:

* Complex performance analytics.
* Social comparison.
* Leaderboards.
* Aggressive streaks.
* Before/after body transformation framing.

## Accessibility considerations

Angela should heavily influence the app’s accessibility baseline.

The app should support:

* Dynamic text scaling.
* Large tap targets.
* Strong contrast.
* Non-colour status indicators.
* Clear focus states.
* Reduced visual clutter.
* Simple navigation.
* Stable layouts at larger font sizes.
* Clear back/navigation behaviour inside the app.

This should not be treated as a special mode. These should be default product qualities.

## Data and trust considerations

Angela needs to know where her data lives and how to recover it. The app should not bury this under generic “settings”.

A clear structure would be:

* Options

  * Appearance
  * Accessibility
  * Data & Backup
  * Units
  * Privacy
  * Help

Data export should be treated as a first-class trust feature, not an advanced utility.

## Product risks

The product will fail Angela if it feels unsafe, visually cramped, or disposable.

It will also fail if it sounds medical without being medically responsible. The app can provide general strength guidance, but it should not pretend to diagnose pain or replace a professional.

## Success metrics

Good signs:

* She completes short routines consistently.
* She uses easier alternatives rather than quitting.
* She records discomfort instead of abandoning the app.
* She increases reps or resistance gradually.
* She can use the app comfortably with larger text.
* She finds backup/export without needing support.
* She keeps using the app after changing devices.

## Validation questions

* What words make her feel encouraged versus patronised?
* How much safety guidance does she want before it feels overwhelming?
* Does she understand exercise alternatives?
* How does she interpret soreness, strain, discomfort, and pain?
* Does she know where she would expect backup/export to live?
* Does the app remain usable at her preferred text size?

## Confidence level

**Medium.** The underlying market and accessibility need are strong, but this persona requires real interviews because safety, pain, ageing, and health confidence are easy to mishandle.

# Working Hypotheses Derived from These Personas

## 1. Hypothesis: A routine-first model will fit most users better than ad-hoc workout assembly

A workout should not exist in isolation. Conceptually, users are executing a routine, adapting it, and building history from repeated sessions.

This supports the model:

**Routine → Workout Session → Exercises → Sets → Logged Performance**

The user should not feel forced to assemble a workout from scratch unless they deliberately choose to.

## 2. Hypothesis: The core value is consistency, not maximal optimisation

The shared need across all three personas is:

**Help me know what to do, do it safely, log it quickly, and return next time without friction.**

This may be more important than advanced analytics, AI coaching, social sharing, or enormous exercise libraries for early adoption.

## 3. Hypothesis: Workout mode and planning mode should be treated differently

Maya needs minimal friction during workout execution. Marcus needs richer tools for planning and editing. Angela needs clarity and safety.

This suggests two distinct UX modes:

**Workout mode**

* Minimal chrome.
* Large controls.
* Clear next action.
* Previous performance visible.
* No navigation noise.
* Fast logging.

**Planning/editing mode**

* More structure.
* More context.
* Routine editing.
* Exercise management.
* History review.
* Settings and data tools.

Design assumption to validate: planning complexity should not leak into active workout logging.

## 4. Hypothesis: Mobile-first is correct, but not mobile-only

The first product should work brilliantly on phones. That is where users will log workouts.

However, routine planning and review may benefit from wider layouts. Desktop/tablet layouts can use sidebars, rails, or split views, while mobile should use simpler root/detail navigation.

## 5. Hypothesis: Navigation should distinguish core destinations from utilities

Core product areas:

* Today / Start Workout
* Routines
* Exercises
* History / Progress

Utility areas:

* Options
* Appearance
* Accessibility
* Data & Backup
* Units
* Privacy
* Help

Design assumption to validate: options should not compete visually with active workout tasks.

## 6. Hypothesis: Bottom nav should disappear or simplify during active workouts

During active workout logging, bottom navigation can become noise. Maya especially needs focus.

Possible rule to test:

* Show main bottom nav on root-level browsing screens.
* Hide or reduce it during active workout mode.
* Use in-app back/close controls for workout subflows.
* Preserve progress if the user accidentally navigates away.

## 7. Hypothesis: Previous performance is a core feature, not a nice-to-have

For both Maya and Marcus, “what did I do last time?” is one of the most important pieces of information.

This should be tested as directly available in the exercise/set logging context.

## 8. Hypothesis: Routine editing should not corrupt history

Marcus makes this crucial.

If a user edits a routine, completed workouts should remain historically accurate. The app should avoid treating past sessions as if they automatically changed with the current routine.

This has important data-model implications to validate in implementation.

## 9. Hypothesis: Accessibility is foundational for all users, not only older users

Angela makes accessibility visible, but Maya and Marcus also benefit from it during real workout conditions.

The app should honour:

* Device text scaling.
* Large tap targets.
* Strong contrast.
* Reduced clutter.
* Non-colour-only feedback.
* Clear focus/active states.
* Stable layouts when text is larger.

Working assumption: this is not polish; it is core usability.

## 10. Hypothesis: Data & Backup deserves a dedicated place

This is the strongest useful contribution from Copilot’s “Data Guardian”.

A workout app accumulates meaningful personal history. Users may train for months or years. Losing that history breaks trust.

Design assumption to test: the app should have a clear **Data & Backup** section, even if the first implementation is simple export/import.

## 11. Hypothesis: The tone should be calm, capable, and gender-neutral

Avoid:

* Beast mode language.
* Shame-based streaks.
* Weight-loss-first framing.
* Gender-coded assumptions.
* Patronising “beginner” language.
* Medical claims.

Prefer:

* “Start workout.”
* “Continue routine.”
* “Last time.”
* “Good form.”
* “Adjust target.”
* “Build consistency.”
* “Logged.”
* “Ready for next set.”

## 12. Hypothesis: The colour palette is strategically aligned

The current palette may work because it avoids two common traps:

* Too masculine/aggressive: black, red, neon, combat-style fitness.
* Too soft/wellness-coded: pastel, decorative, vague.

The indigo action scale supports focus, structure, and progression. The mint accent scale supports calm, recovery, trust, and inclusiveness.

This suggests the palette could be suitable for a broad English-speaking audience across sex and gender, pending validation.

## 13. Hypothesis: The MVP should be narrower than the app’s eventual concept

A candidate MVP focus to validate:

* Create or accept a default routine.
* Start workout.
* Log sets/reps/weight quickly.
* See previous performance.
* Finish workout.
* Review simple history.
* Edit routine.
* Export/import data.
* Honour text scaling and accessible layouts.

Defer:

* AI coaching.
* Social sharing.
* Wearable sync.
* Advanced programme cycles.
* Public exercise marketplace.
* Body transformation tracking.
* Calorie tracking.

## 14. The biggest product risk is building an admin tool instead of a workout companion

The app can have structured data underneath, but the user should mostly experience clarity.

The product should feel like:

**“I know what I’m doing today, and the app is helping me do it.”**

Not:

**“I have to maintain a fitness database before I can exercise.”**

# Main Uncertainties to Validate

## Audience priority

The biggest open question is which persona should dominate the first release:

* Maya gives the clearest mainstream mobile MVP.
* Marcus gives the strongest architecture and long-term depth.
* Angela gives the strongest accessibility, safety, and trust requirements.

A good first version should primarily serve Maya while not blocking Marcus and not excluding Angela.

## Home versus gym emphasis

The app can support both, but the first experience should make a choice. Home/basic-gym strength training is probably the clearest starting point.

## Routine flexibility

Users may think differently about routine changes. Some will think in versions; others will think “this is just my routine now”. The app should probably preserve history internally without forcing versioning language onto casual users.

## Progress framing

Need to test whether users prefer progress framed as:

* Strength increase.
* Consistency.
* Completed volume.
* Exercise confidence.
* Body change.
* Health/mobility.

The safest default is consistency + performance, not body weight.

## Safety language

Need to test wording around soreness, strain, joint pain, and discomfort. This must be useful without becoming medical advice.

## Backup expectations

Need to learn whether early users expect:

* Local export only.
* Cloud sync.
* Account-based backup.
* Apple/Google integration.
* CSV/JSON export.

For an MVP, visible export/import may be enough to establish trust.

# Suggested Interview Targets

Interview 5–8 people before locking the core IA:

1. Women aged 25–40 who strength train casually or want to start.
2. Men aged 30–45 who already edit routines or track lifts.
3. Adults aged 50–65 who want strength for health, mobility, or ageing well.
4. At least one person who uses large text or accessibility settings.
5. At least one person who has lost fitness data or switched workout apps.

Best questions:

* “What made you stop using your last workout app?”
* “What do you need to see during an actual set?”
* “What do you hate entering manually?”
* “What does progress mean to you?”
* “When does a reminder feel helpful, and when does it feel judgmental?”
* “What would make you trust this app with months of workout history?”
* “Where would you expect backup/export to live?”
* “What would make the app feel like it was not made for someone like you?”
